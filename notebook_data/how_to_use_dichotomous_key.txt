HOW TO USE A NUMBERED-LEAD DICHOTOMOUS IDENTIFICATION KEY
==========================================================

WHAT IS A DICHOTOMOUS KEY?
---------------------------
A dichotomous key is a tool for identifying an unknown organism by answering
a series of two-choice questions (dichotomous = "dividing into two"). At each
step you are presented with exactly two contrasting statements; you choose the
one that matches your specimen, which then directs you to the next pair of
statements. You repeat this until you arrive at an identification.

This type of key is widely used in biology (insects, plants, fungi, birds,
fish, etc.) and follows an internationally recognised format.


THE NUMBERED-LEAD FORMAT
-------------------------
Each statement in the key is called a "lead". In the numbered-lead format,
every lead has a unique number and the key is written as follows:

    n (m): [descriptive text about the feature to observe]

    n  = the number of this lead
    m  = the number of the paired/alternative lead (its sibling)
    text = a description of the observable character

Leads n and m together form one "couplet" — a pair of contrasting statements.
You will always choose exactly one lead from each couplet.

The second lead of a couplet (the sibling) generally does NOT carry a
parenthetical number of its own; its partner is identified by the first
lead's parenthetical. For example:

    5 (12): Wings uniform pale yellow.
    ...
    12: Wings with dark spots or bands.

Here lead 5 and lead 12 form a couplet. Lead 5 says "(12)", identifying
lead 12 as its alternative.

A lead that ends with a taxon name (species, genus, etc.) is a TERMINAL
lead — it is the final identification result, not a pointer forward.

A lead that ends without a taxon name is an INTERMEDIATE lead — it directs
you to continue; the next couplet to evaluate starts immediately after in
the numbered sequence.


HOW TO USE THE KEY — STEP BY STEP
-----------------------------------
1. PREPARE YOUR SPECIMEN OR IMAGE
   Examine your specimen carefully. Good lighting and magnification (a hand
   lens or microscope where needed) will help you observe fine details.

2. START AT THE FIRST COUPLET
   Always begin at couplet 1 (the lead numbered "1"). The key is not meant
   to be entered at a random point.

3. READ BOTH LEADS IN THE COUPLET
   Never decide on the basis of the first lead alone. Read lead n AND lead m
   before making your choice.

4. CHOOSE THE MATCHING LEAD
   Select the lead whose description best matches your specimen. If you are
   uncertain, note both possibilities and follow each in turn (see the tip
   on running the key twice below).

5. FOLLOW THE DIRECTION
   - If the chosen lead is an INTERMEDIATE lead, the next couplet begins at
     the lead that immediately follows in numerical order within the sub-key
     you have entered.
   - If the chosen lead is a TERMINAL lead, you have reached an
     identification. Verify it against an image or description of that taxon.

6. VERIFY YOUR IDENTIFICATION
   Always confirm the result. Compare your specimen with a reference image,
   description, or voucher specimen to ensure it matches the identified taxon
   in all respects.


TIPS FOR ACCURATE RESULTS
--------------------------
- Observe the specimen before reading any leads. Let the character of the
  specimen guide your choice, not wishful matching to a name you already
  have in mind.

- If a character is ambiguous or damaged, run the key both ways and see
  which terminal lead produces a plausible match when verified.

- Terminology in older keys may differ from modern usage. Check the glossary
  or terms section of the publication the key comes from.

- Some characters (colour, size, androconia, etc.) are sex-dependent or
  population-variable. Note the sex and origin of your specimen before
  starting.

- Upperside vs. underside: many keys distinguish characters visible only
  from one surface. Know which surface your photograph or specimen shows.


UNDERSTANDING THE STRUCTURE OF THE KEY
---------------------------------------
Dichotomous keys have a tree structure. Each couplet splits the remaining
candidate taxa into two groups. Choosing a lead enters you into one branch
of that tree and excludes the other.

The full pathway from the start to a terminal lead — the ordered list of
lead numbers you passed through — is called the IDENTIFICATION PATH or
KEY PATH for that taxon. For example, a path of "1 · 5 · 12 · 20 · 21"
means you chose lead 1, then lead 5, then lead 12, then lead 20, and
reached species identification at lead 21.

To trace the path for a known taxon, look up which lead is terminal for
that species and work backwards through the parenthetical numbers.


WORKED EXAMPLE (generic)
--------------------------
Suppose a simplified key reads:

    1 (8):   Legs six.
    2 (5):   Wings present.
    3 (4):   Two pairs of wings.
    4:       One pair of wings. ... Order Diptera (flies)
    5:       Wings absent.
    6 (7):   Body divided into head, thorax, abdomen; antennae present. ... Class Insecta (wingless insect)
    7:       Body not divided into three regions; no antennae. ... Class Arachnida
    8:       Legs eight. ... Class Arachnida

Step 1 — Couplet {1, 8}
  Lead 1: "Legs six."
  Lead 8: "Legs eight."
  Specimen has SIX legs. → CHOOSE LEAD 1.

Step 2 — Couplet {2, 5}  [sub-key following lead 1]
  Lead 2: "Wings present."
  Lead 5: "Wings absent."
  Specimen has wings. → CHOOSE LEAD 2.

Step 3 — Couplet {3, 4}
  Lead 3: "Two pairs of wings."
  Lead 4: "One pair of wings. ... Order Diptera"
  Specimen has ONE pair of wings. → CHOOSE LEAD 4.
  → RESULT: Order Diptera (flies)  ✓

Key path followed: leads 1 · 2 · 4


COMMON PROBLEMS AND HOW TO HANDLE THEM
----------------------------------------
PROBLEM: Both leads in a couplet seem to match (or neither does).
SOLUTION: The character may be variable, damaged, or sex-dependent. Try
both branches independently and see which terminal lead verifies correctly
against a reference image.

PROBLEM: The taxon I think I have is not in the key.
SOLUTION: The key covers only the taxa in its scope. If your specimen is
not from that region, time period, or group, the key cannot identify it.
Check that your specimen falls within the key's stated coverage.

PROBLEM: I do not understand a technical term.
SOLUTION: Look up the term in the glossary of the source publication, or in
a standard entomological/botanical/zoological dictionary. Do not substitute
a common-language interpretation — specialised terms in keys are precise.

PROBLEM: The features needed are not visible from my photograph.
SOLUTION: Some characters require a physical specimen or a specific view.
Features marked ♂ (male) or ♀ (female) require knowing the sex of the
specimen. If a character cannot be assessed, skip that couplet and try
alternative characters if the key provides them, or seek a more complete
specimen.


GLOSSARY OF COMMON KEY NOTATION
---------------------------------
♂  — male specimen
♀  — female specimen
cf. — compare with (uncertain resemblance)
n.sp. — new (undescribed) species
agg. — aggregate (a group of closely similar taxa treated together)
s.l. — sensu lato (in the broad sense)
s.str. — sensu stricto (in the strict sense)
ssp. / subsp. — subspecies
fwl — forewing length (tip of wing to base, excluding fringe)
HW  — hindwing
FW  — forewing
→   — "leads to" / "result is"
...  — descriptive text continues / terminal name follows


CITATION AND PROVENANCE
------------------------
Always record which key you used (author, title, publication, year, edition)
alongside your identification. Different keys for the same group may use
different taxonomic concepts or regional scope, and a key-based identification
is only as reliable as the key itself.
