HOW TO USE THE JAMIDES DICHOTOMOUS IDENTIFICATION KEY (keys.txt)
=================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
keys.txt is a dichotomous (two-choice) key for identifying Malaysian
Jamides (Caerulean) butterflies. At each step you choose between exactly
two numbered statements that describe an observable feature; one statement
matches your specimen, the other does not. Following the chosen statement
leads you to either the next pair of choices or directly to a species name.

The key covers 19 species (and one out-of-range Sumatran species, J. lucide,
included for completeness). It is adapted from the treatment in Corner &
Penney and is reproduced here in a compact numbered-lead format.


UNDERSTANDING THE LEAD FORMAT
------------------------------
Each line in the key is called a "lead". Leads are numbered sequentially
and written as:

    n (m): [descriptive text]

    n  = the number of this lead
    m  = the number of the PAIRED alternative lead (its sibling)
    text = a description of the feature to observe

Together, leads n and m form one couplet — a pair of contrasting statements.
Exactly one will match your specimen; the other will not.

A lead that ends with a species name in the form "... Jamides species" is a
TERMINAL lead — it is the final identification, not a pointer to more choices.

A lead that ends without a species name is an INTERMEDIATE lead — it points
you forward; the next couplet to consider is the one that immediately follows
in the numbered sequence (the lowest-numbered lead in the text after the
current one that you have not yet visited).


STEP-BY-STEP INSTRUCTIONS
--------------------------
1. Start at couplet {1, 21} — the very first pair in the key.
   Read BOTH leads 1 and 21 carefully.

2. Decide which lead matches your specimen.
   - If lead 1 matches → remain in the sub-key that begins at the next
     listed lead (which is lead 2).
   - If lead 21 matches → skip to lead 21's sub-key (which begins at
     lead 22).

3. At the next couplet, again read both alternatives and choose the one
   that matches.

4. Continue until you reach a terminal lead — one that ends with a
   species name. That is your identification.

IMPORTANT: At each couplet you must read BOTH options before deciding.
Never stop reading at the first lead that seems plausible.


READING THE PAIRED STRUCTURE
-----------------------------
The "(m)" parenthetical tells you which lead is the alternative.

Example:
    2 (7): Underside forewing postdiscal band slightly dislocated at vein 6...
    ...
    7: Underside forewing postdiscal band continuous from vein 3 to vein 7...

Leads 2 and 7 are a pair. If lead 2 matches your specimen, proceed to the
sub-key starting at lead 3. If lead 7 matches, proceed to the sub-key
starting at lead 8.

Note that the second lead in a couplet (the higher-numbered one, e.g. lead 7)
does not carry a parenthetical — its partner is implicit from the first lead's
parenthetical: lead 2 says "(7)", so the pair is {2, 7}.


WORKED EXAMPLE — identifying Jamides caeruleus
-----------------------------------------------
Specimen: Jamides caeruleus (Sky Blue), collected in Peninsular Malaysia.

Step 1 — Couplet {1, 21}
  Lead 1: "Underside forewing postdiscal band in space 3 more or less in
           line with the band in spaces 4 and 5."
  Lead 21: "Underside forewing postdiscal band completely dislocated at
            vein 4..."
  → Band in space 3 is clearly shifted inward (dislocated). CHOOSE LEAD 21.

Step 2 — Couplet {22, 37}  [follow lead 21's sub-key, which starts at 22]
  Lead 22: "Underside all white striae clearly defined."
  Lead 37: "Underside postdiscal and submarginal striae so broad... mostly white."
  → Striae are clearly defined. CHOOSE LEAD 22.

Step 3 — Couplet {23, 36}
  Lead 23: "Underside forewing postdiscal band slightly dislocated at vein 6..."
  Lead 36: "Underside forewing postdiscal band slightly curved but not
            dislocated at vein 6. ♂ upperside green..."
  → Band is also shifted at vein 6 (not green-uppersided). CHOOSE LEAD 23.

Step 4 — Couplet {24, 27}
  Lead 24: "Underside hindwing striae arranged so that there is a more or
            less continuous pair of subbasal white lines."
  Lead 27: "Underside hindwing subbasal striae dislocated."
  → Subbasal striae are dislocated. CHOOSE LEAD 27.

Step 5 — Couplet {28, 29}
  Lead 28: "Underside forewing usually with a pair of costal white dashes...
            Small, fwl less than 15.0 mm."
  Lead 29: "Underside forewing without costal dashes... Larger, fwl normally
            more than 15.0 mm."
  → No costal dashes; forewing larger than 15 mm. CHOOSE LEAD 29.

Step 6 — Couplet {30, 31}
  Lead 30: "Underside hindwing with the second white stria from the base in
            space 7 in line with the third stria from the base in the cell...
            ♂ upperside metallic caerulean blue... → Jamides caeruleus"
  Lead 31: "Underside hindwing with the second white stria from the base in
            space 7 about midway between the second and third striae from the
            base in the cell."
  → Stria in space 7 is in line with the third cell stria. CHOOSE LEAD 30.
  → RESULT: Jamides caeruleus  ✓

Key path followed: leads 21 · 22 · 23 · 27 · 29 · 30


SYMBOLS USED IN THE KEY
------------------------
♂   — male
♀   — female
fwl — forewing length (measured from base to apex)
HW  — hindwing
FW  — forewing
→   — "leads to" or "identification is"
...  — continued text (line wrapped for readability)


UPPERSIDE FEATURES
------------------
Several couplets (especially K8–K11 and K15, K18–K19 in the tool's
numbering) rely primarily on upperside characters — male wing colour,
border width, androconia, hindwing submarginal markings, female upperside
transparency. These features require a specimen or a photo showing the
upperside. If you only have an underside photograph, you may not be able
to resolve these couplets, but underside features alone are often sufficient
to narrow the identification considerably.

The id_keys.html scoring tool provides a "Skip — upperside feature" button
for couplets that depend primarily on the upperside.


COVERAGE AND SCOPE
------------------
This key covers the 19 Jamides species recorded from Malaysia (Peninsular
Malaysia + Sabah/Sarawak). It does not cover:
- Jamides lucide (Sumatra only; included in the original key for completeness
  but not recorded from Malaysia)
- Jamides species from outside the Malaysian region
- Undescribed or recently described taxa not in the original C&P treatment


READING ORDER SUMMARY OF THE 19 COUPLETS
-----------------------------------------
K1  {1,  21} — FW band in space 3: in line vs. dislocated at vein 4
K2  {2,   7} — FW band at vein 6: slightly dislocated vs. continuous
K3  {3,   4} — Underside colour: greyish-brown buff striae vs. paler grey white striae
K4  {5,   6} — HW submarginal spaces: strongly darkened vs. barely darker
K5  {8,   9} — ♂ underside: atypical white-on-black (yehi) vs. typical
K6  {9,  18} — HW submarginal spaces: not blackened vs. wedge-shaped black spots
K7  {10, 15} — HW striae: subbasal pair only vs. subbasal + discal pairs
K8  {11, 12} — ♂ upperside: whitish blue broad border vs. pale blue narrow border
K9  {13, 14} — ♂ upperside: unicolorous thread border (pura) vs. deepening blue (zebra)
K10 {16, 17} — ♂ upperside: deep sky blue + HW spots (malaccanus) vs. paler no spots
K11 {19, 20} — ♂ upperside: sky blue thread border (philatus) vs. almost white (aratus)
K12 {22, 37} — Striae: all clearly defined vs. extremely broad (lucide, out of range)
K13 {23, 36} — FW band: dislocated at vein 6 vs. only curved (♂ green = abdul)
K14 {24, 27} — HW subbasal striae: continuous pair vs. dislocated
K15 {25, 26} — ♂ upperside: purple-blue no androconia + costal dashes (cunilda) vs. paler + androconia (ferrari)
K16 {28, 29} — FW costal dashes + size: small with dashes (talinga) vs. larger without
K17 {30, 31} — HW stria 7: in line with 3rd cell stria (caeruleus) vs. midway
K18 {32, 35} — ♂ upperside border: thread no HW marks (elpis/limes) vs. ~1mm + HW marks (alecto)
K19 {33, 34} — Size + striae: smaller narrow (elpis) vs. larger diffuse Borneo (limes)
